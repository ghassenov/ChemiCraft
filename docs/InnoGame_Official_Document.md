# Specifications

## 1 — Context and Challenges

Science is at the heart of contemporary issues: understanding matter, protecting the environment, designing sustainable materials, analyzing motion, or explaining light. Yet certain concepts remain difficult to convey in a concrete and engaging way to young audiences.

InNOGame proposes using games as a tool for science popularization. Teams must transform scientific concepts into playable, testable, and reusable experiences for science clubs, high schools, and universities.

The mission is not to produce a simple teaching aid, but to design a game or gamified prototype that fosters active learning, curiosity, experimentation, cooperation, and scientific understanding.

---

## 2 — Core Problem Statement

*"How can we design educational games capable of making scientific concepts more accessible, engaging, and memorable for young people aged 16 to 25, while ensuring scientific accuracy, pedagogical quality, and the playability of the prototype?"*

---

## 3 — Thematic Axes

Each team chooses one or more of the following axes.

### 3.1. Molecular Cuisine and Chemical Attraction

How to explain culinary transformations, molecular interactions, and chemical reactions through a game experience?
- Reactions between ingredients, pH, emulsions, gelification, foam, crystallization.
- Ideas: recipe-molecule cards, mini culinary laboratory, scientific recipe escape game.

### 3.2. Environment and Climate Change

How to raise awareness about climate and environmental issues through choices, scenarios, and visible consequences within the game?
- Greenhouse effect, pollution, carbon footprint, biodiversity, resources, renewable energy.
- Ideas: sustainable city, climate crisis simulation, cooperative impact-reduction game (carbon footprint, water footprint...).

### 3.3. Materials and Recycling

How to convey the properties of materials, their life cycle, and the limits of recycling?
- Plastic, glass, metal, paper, biomaterials, sorting, reuse, circular economy.
- Ideas: smart sorting game, waste lifecycle, gamified design of a sustainable object.

### 3.4. Magnetic Fields, Speed, and Motion

How to illustrate forces, speed, motion, and magnetic phenomena through interactive challenges?
- Attraction, repulsion, trajectory, speed, acceleration, forces, physical applications.
- Ideas: science race, magnetic board game, mini motion simulation, trajectory challenges.

### 3.5. Optics and Colors

How to make light, color, reflection, refraction, and visual perception phenomena visible?
- Light propagation, filters, colors, optical illusions, eye and perception.
- Ideas: color-filter escape game, light puzzle, ray game, interactive visual quiz.

---

## 4 — The Product to Develop

The goal is to design and prototype an educational or scientific game. It can be physical, digital, or hybrid. Teams are free to propose any form of game, provided the prototype is demonstrable and scientific learning is clearly integrated.

### 4.1. Expected Formats – Mandatory Prerequisite

The solution must correspond to at least one primary format and clearly cover a pedagogical objective. This requirement is verified before grading; its absence may cap the score at **50 / 100**.

| Format | Description |
|---|---|
| **Physical game** | Card game, board game, hands-on material, paper puzzles. |
| **Digital game** | Mini-game, interactive quiz, simple simulation, application, or web prototype. |
| **Serious game** | Educational scenario with objectives, progression, roles, and decisions. |
| **Escape game** | Series of scientific puzzles leading to a final resolution. |

---

## 5 — Golden Rules

### 5.1. Scientific Accuracy
- Every integrated scientific concept must be correct, verifiable, and simply explained.
- Approximations must be declared and adapted to the target audience's level.

### 5.2. Real Gamification
- The project must not be a simple presentation.
- It must contain game mechanics: challenge, score, progression, cooperation, competition, choices, or puzzles.

### 5.3. Pedagogical Accessibility
- Rules, objectives, and concepts must be understandable by young people aged 13 to 25.

### 5.4. Prototype Feasibility
- The prototype must be achievable within the hackathon timeframe and demonstrable during the final pitch.

---

## 6 — Practical and Technical Recommendations

- **Physical formats:** cards, board, tokens, mission sheets, puzzles, rapid prototyping materials.
- **Digital formats:** Scratch, Godot, Unity, Construct, Genially, Kahoot, Figma, Canva, simple web prototype.
- **Pedagogical approach:** clear learning objective, short rules, user testing, simplified documentation.
- **Scientific approach:** reliable sources, validated concepts, concrete examples, no pseudoscience.

---

## 7 — Mandatory Deliverables

All deliverables below are mandatory. The absence of a major deliverable may cap the score at **50 / 100**.

| # | Deliverable | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 7.1 | **Demonstrable prototype** | Testable version of the game: physical, digital, or hybrid. A simple non-playable idea is not accepted. |
| 7.2 | **Game rules** | Number of players, duration, materials, gameplay, possible actions, victory conditions. |
| 7.3 | **Pedagogical and scientific note** | Concepts covered, learning objectives, target level, sources, limitations, and simplifications. |
| 7.4 | **Pitch** | 15 minutes total: 10 min presentation + 5 min Q&A. |

---

## 8 — Evaluation Criteria

Projects are evaluated according to the criteria below. The detailed rubric may be provided separately to the jury.

| # | Criterion | Weight | Points |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Scientific quality** | 25% | / 25 |
| 2 | **Pedagogical quality & impact** | 25% | / 25 |
| 3 | **Playability & user experience** | 25% | / 25 |
| 4 | **Innovation & feasibility** | 25% | / 25 |

**Key aspects:**
1. Accuracy, coherence, correct popularization.
2. Learning objective, target audience, reusability.
3. Rules, interaction, fun, design, testing.
4. Originality, prototype, realism, pitch.

**Bonus – +5:** For physical versions: bonus for using sustainable materials (e.g., wood) or recycled materials (toilet paper rolls, bottle caps, bottles...).

---

## 9 — Non-Compliance Clauses

The penalties below are cumulative. In the event of a critical red flag, the score may be capped at **40 / 100**.

| Penalty | Clause |
|---|---|
| **-15 pts** | Absence of a playable prototype: project limited to an idea or presentation. |
| **-15 pts** | False, misleading, or unverified scientific content. |
| **-10 pts** | Game not understandable by the target audience or overly vague rules. |
| **-10 pts** | Weak game dimension: absence of clear game mechanics. |
| **-10 pts** | Pedagogical documentation absent or insufficient. |
| **-10 pts** | Prototype unachievable within the timeframe or not demonstrable before the jury. |

---

## 10 — Hackathon Vision

InNOGame aims to build a new generation of educational resources where science becomes more accessible, more concrete, and more engaging. The hackathon encourages young people to learn by creating, to popularize by playing, and to transform scientific concepts into shareable experiences.

The best projects may be showcased in science clubs, workshops, educational events, open days, or science mediation activities. The ambition is to produce useful, improvable, and reusable prototypes after the hackathon.

> *"Science becomes more alive when it is manipulated, questioned, and played with."*
